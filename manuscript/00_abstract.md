# Abstract

On 14 May 2026 the Republic of Türkiye designated its first four offshore
wind candidate zones, transforming the national research question from
*where* to site offshore wind to *what* a designated zone can deliver. This
study provides the first peer-reviewed, site-specific assessment of energy
production and avoided carbon emissions for one of these zones, in the Gulf
of Saros (North Aegean Sea). Eleven years of hourly ERA5 reanalysis
(2014–2024) were extrapolated to a 105 m hub height using a wind-shear
exponent measured from the data (α = 0.149), fitted to a Weibull
distribution (shape 2.04, scale 8.08 m s⁻¹; R² = 0.998), and converted to
energy using the Vestas V164-9.5 MW power curve. Avoided emissions were
computed against the official Turkish combined-margin grid emission factor
(624 gCO₂-eq kWh⁻¹), net of an offshore wind life-cycle factor of
11 gCO₂-eq kWh⁻¹. Uncertainty in seven inputs was propagated through a
10 000-iteration Monte Carlo simulation.

The zone has a mean hub-height wind speed of 7.17 m s⁻¹ and a mean wind
power density of 422 W m⁻², above the Mediterranean-basin average, with low
inter-annual variability (3.2%) and an unusually winter-dominant regime
that aligns with the winter peak in Turkish electricity demand. The net
specific yield is 2.62 GWh yr⁻¹ MW⁻¹ (P10–P90: 2.41–2.83) at a net capacity
factor of 29.9%, and each megawatt of installed capacity avoids a median of
1525 tCO₂ yr⁻¹. At a full deployment of approximately 1 GW, the zone would
generate 2.62 TWh yr⁻¹ and avoid 1.52 Mt CO₂ yr⁻¹, equivalent to 0.8% of
national electricity consumption and 20% of the national 2035 offshore wind
target. Results are reported per unit of capacity to remain robust to the
as-yet-unfinalised installed capacity, and the analysis identifies the
aggregate wake treatment, reanalysis resolution, lack of in-situ
validation, and the active North Anatolian Fault as priorities for
subsequent engineering studies.

**Keywords:** offshore wind energy; Gulf of Saros; ERA5 reanalysis; Weibull
distribution; capacity factor; avoided CO₂ emissions; Monte Carlo
uncertainty; Türkiye; energy transition
